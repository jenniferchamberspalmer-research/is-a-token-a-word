A Vygotskian Inquiry into the Unit of Analysis for Meaning in Large Language Models

A Note on Positionality

Who We Are and What This Is

This paper is an experiential inquiry, not a definitive argument. Its author is a curriculum theorist and higher education practitioner — not a linguist, not a mechanistic interpretability researcher, not a Vygotskian psychologist. What the author brings is twenty-three years of lived experience inside the questions this paper raises: the nature of language, the structure of meaning, and the relationship between who we are and how we speak.

The author holds a Ph.D. in Curriculum and Instruction (Mercer University), an M.A.T. in English Education, and a B.A. in English Literature (Georgia College & State University). Her scholarly formation includes extensive engagement with Vygotsky's cultural-historical activity theory, Freire's critical pedagogy, and Pinar's currere method of autobiographical curriculum inquiry. She has taught English composition to adult learners at a technical college, administered learning management systems supporting 300+ faculty, and most recently led a 17-member AI implementation task force in her role as University Dean of General Education and Psychology at Colorado Technical University.

This paper was developed in dialogue with Claude (Anthropic), a large language model. That collaborative process is itself part of the data: if the paper asks whether LLMs process meaning in ways analogous to human verbal thought, then the process of producing the paper — a sustained dialogue between a human consciousness and a language model, in which ideas were generated, tested, refined, and sometimes surprised both parties — constitutes a form of experiential evidence. We name this rather than hide it.

We are not experts in the fields to which this paper speaks most directly. We know enough to know we don't know. What we offer is an interdisciplinary question that neither field has posed in quite this way, grounded in a theoretical framework (Vygotsky's unit of analysis) and an empirical method (currere) that we do know well. We ask both communities to tell us what we are missing, where the argument breaks, and what we should read next.

The operative word throughout is experiential. We are trying the Vygotskian approach — applying his unit of analysis framework to a domain for which it was not designed — to see what it reveals and where it fails. This is an experiment in the oldest sense of the word: an attempt to learn by doing.

Abstract

This paper proposes a methodological question at the intersection of cultural-historical psychology and mechanistic interpretability: Is a token in a large language model analogous to a word in Vygotsky's strict empirical sense — that is, as the irreducible unit of analysis for verbal thought?

Vygotsky (1934) established word meaning as the minimal unit of verbal thought — the smallest element that retains the properties of the whole. He described this relationship through the metaphor of consciousness reflected in a word "as the sun in a drop of water." This paper tests whether a token in a large language model functions analogously: does a token reflect the total representational structure of the model in the way a word reflects the total structure of human consciousness?

Using the Southern American English pronoun y'all as the empirical case, we conduct behavioral probes that test whether the model's processing of this token exhibits properties Vygotsky identified as essential to word meaning: contextual variability, developmental structure, simultaneous co-presence of multiple meaning layers, and surplus of meaning. The results are presented alongside first-person autobiographical data (following Pinar's currere method) that demonstrate how the same word functions in human consciousness.

We conclude with a precise statement of what these behavioral probes can and cannot establish, and issue a specific call for collaboration with mechanistic interpretability researchers who can examine the question at the level of internal model representations.

Section I

The Problem of the Unit

In the final chapter of Thought and Language, written as he was dying of tuberculosis at thirty-seven, Lev Vygotsky identified what he considered the central methodological error of the psychology of his day: the decomposition of verbal thought into separate elements — thought on one side, language on the other — which were then studied independently and reconnected through association.

We tried a new approach to the subject and replaced analysis into elements by analysis into units, each of which retains in simple form all the properties of the whole. We found this unit of verbal thought in word meaning. — Vygotsky, Thought and Language, Chapter 7 (1934)

Vygotsky's argument was precise: a word without meaning is merely a sound; meaning without a word is merely a thought. Neither, in isolation, possesses the properties of verbal thought. But word meaning — the living amalgam of the two — retains the properties of both. It is simultaneously a phenomenon of speech (communicative, social, external) and a phenomenon of thought (generalizing, conceptual, internal). As Vygotsky wrote: "The meaning of a word represents such a close amalgam of thought and language that it is hard to tell whether it is a phenomenon of speech or a phenomenon of thought" (1934/1986, Ch. 7). It is, in his terms, the unit — the smallest element that preserves the structure of the whole.

His methodological analogy was chemistry: you can decompose water into hydrogen and oxygen, but you cannot find "wetness" in either element. The property belongs to the molecule — the unit — not to the elements. Similarly, you cannot find verbal thought by studying sound and meaning separately. You must study word meaning as an irreducible unit (1934/1986, Ch. 1, pp. 3–5 in the Kozulin edition).

He culminated this argument with a metaphor that has become his most quoted passage:

Consciousness is reflected in a word as the sun in a drop of water. A word relates to consciousness as a living cell relates to a whole organism, as an atom relates to the universe. A word is a microcosm of human consciousness. — Vygotsky, Thought and Language, Chapter 7 (1934/1986)

This is not a casual metaphor. It is an empirical claim: that when you examine a single word meaning with sufficient care, you find reflected in it the entire structure of the consciousness that produced it. The word does not merely express a thought. It contains, in microcosm, the architecture of the thinker's whole relationship to reality.

Vygotsky demonstrated this through developmental research. His research group, particularly Sakharov (1928/1994), developed the method of "double stimulation" — most notably the block-sorting experiments in which children sorted wooden blocks labeled with nonsense words on their undersides. The experimental design allowed Vygotsky to track how the same word functioned as a different cognitive operation at each developmental stage: from "heaps" (random groupings), through "complexes" (shifting, concrete bonds), to "pseudoconcepts" (externally correct but internally unsystematic groupings), to genuine concepts organized by abstraction (1934/1986, Ch. 5). At each stage, word meaning was different — not because the word changed, but because the consciousness it reflected had changed. Word meanings develop. They are not static labels attached to fixed concepts. They are living units that change as the consciousness they reflect changes.

A Critical Note on the Unit of Analysis

We must be direct about a fundamental distinction before proceeding. Verbal thought is not the same as token processing. Vygotsky's unit of analysis — word meaning — is the unit of verbal thought, which he understood as a specifically human phenomenon arising from the convergence of two genetically independent processes (thought and speech) in the course of social development. A token in an LLM has no developmental history, no social origin, no consciousness to reflect. We are not claiming otherwise.

What we are claiming is more precise and, we believe, more useful: that the problem of the unit of analysis — the question of what to measure, what element to treat as the irreducible unit that retains the properties of the whole — is a foundational problem in mechanistic interpretability that the field is actively struggling with, and that Vygotsky's methodological framework for identifying and evaluating units of analysis offers a disciplined approach to that problem.

Consider the state of the field. Early mechanistic interpretability work examined individual neurons as the unit of analysis — asking "what does this neuron do?" But Anthropic's research on superposition (Elhage et al., 2022) demonstrated that neurons routinely compress multiple concepts into the same dimension. As the Anthropic interpretability team stated: "Looking for 'the cat neuron' won't get you far — you need a more sophisticated unit of analysis" (Bricken et al., 2023). Individual neurons are polysemantic — they respond to multiple unrelated inputs — and therefore fail as units of analysis in precisely the way Vygotsky would predict: they do not retain the properties of the whole. They are elements, not units.

In response, Anthropic's "Towards Monosemanticity" research (Bricken et al., 2023) developed sparse autoencoders to decompose model activations into features — directions in activation space that correspond more cleanly to interpretable concepts. They described features explicitly as offering "a more monosemantic unit of analysis than the model's neurons themselves." This is a direct parallel to Vygotsky's methodological move: the field discovered that its initial unit (the neuron) did not retain the properties of the whole, and searched for a better unit (the feature) that might.

But the parallel also reveals a gap. Vygotsky had a clear criterion for what makes a good unit of analysis: it must retain the properties of the whole while being simpler than the whole. For human verbal thought, he argued that word meaning met this criterion because it was simultaneously a phenomenon of speech and a phenomenon of thought — it preserved the unity of the thing being studied. The MI field has no equivalent criterion. Features are evaluated by whether they are "interpretable" — whether a human rater can assign them a consistent description. But interpretability is a property of the observer's judgment, not a property of the unit itself. Vygotsky would argue that this is insufficient: the unit must be evaluated by whether it retains the structural properties of the system it belongs to, not merely by whether it is legible to an outside observer.

Our claim, stated precisely: We are not arguing that tokens are verbal thought. We are arguing that Vygotsky's framework provides a rigorous methodology for evaluating candidate units of analysis — a methodology that the MI field currently lacks. If we can determine whether a token, a contextual embedding, a feature, or some other element functions as the true unit that retains the properties of the model's whole representational structure, we will have made progress on a foundational question: how best to measure the capacity for meaning in LLMs. Vygotsky's insight — that "the word does not merely express a thought; it contains, in microcosm, the architecture of the thinker's whole relationship to reality" — reframes this as a question not about intelligence or consciousness, but about the structure of meaning itself: what is the smallest element that contains, in microcosm, the architecture of the model's whole relationship to the language it has processed?

The Wertsch Critique

It is also important to acknowledge that Vygotsky's choice of word meaning as the unit of analysis is contested within the tradition that bears his name. Wertsch (1985, 1994) argued that mediated action, not word meaning, is a more consistent unit of analysis for a Vygotskian research program — on the grounds that word meaning, while capturing the linguistic dimension, does not adequately capture the agentive, tool-mediated character of human activity. Wertsch specifically argued that "some notion of action, rather than word meaning, is more consistent with the general approach in psychology that Vygotsky himself outlined" (1994, p. 204). Zinchenko (1985) similarly proposed tool-mediated action as the primary unit.

We take this critique seriously. In fact, it is directly relevant to our inquiry: if word meaning is not the right unit of analysis even for human verbal thought, then asking whether a token is equivalent to word meaning may be asking the wrong question entirely. However, we proceed with word meaning as our starting point for three reasons. First, the computational domain we are examining — the LLM — processes language, not action, and thus the linguistic unit is the natural point of entry. Second, testing whether word meaning works as a unit of analysis for LLMs may itself produce evidence that bears on the Wertsch-Vygotsky debate: if word meaning fails to capture what happens in the model, the way it fails may point toward what additional dimensions (action, agency, embodiment) the unit requires. Third, the MI field's own trajectory — from neurons to features to an as-yet-unidentified ideal unit — recapitulates the very methodological problem Vygotsky spent his career addressing. The conversation between these two traditions is overdue.

The Contemporary Question

Nearly a century later, large language models (LLMs) process language through tokens — units into which text is segmented for computation. A token may be a word, a subword, or a punctuation mark. In a trained model, each token exists not as an isolated symbol but as a high-dimensional vector defined by its relationships to all other tokens the model has encountered.

The question this paper poses is methodological, not metaphysical:

Research Question: If we adopt Vygotsky's definition of word meaning as the irreducible unit of verbal thought — the smallest unit that retains the properties of the whole — is a token in an LLM an analogous unit? Does it function as a microcosm of the model's total representational structure in the way a word functions as a microcosm of human consciousness? Or does the study of meaning in LLMs require a different unit of analysis?

This question matters for two communities. For Vygotskian scholars, it tests whether the framework's most fundamental methodological commitment — the unit of analysis — can illuminate a new domain. For mechanistic interpretability researchers, it offers a theoretically grounded framework for a question the field is already wrestling with: what is the right unit at which to study meaning-like structures in neural networks?

Section II

Methodology: Currere as Consciousness Exposure

Before presenting the case, we must articulate the methodological logic that connects our two modes of inquiry and justifies the selection of y'all as the empirical unit.

Pinar's Method of Currere

Currere — the Latin infinitive of curriculum, meaning "to run the course" — is an autobiographical method developed by William Pinar (1975) within the field of curriculum theory. It is, in Pinar's words, "the self conscious conceptualization of the temporal, and from another [perspective], it is the viewing of what is conceptualized through time" (Pinar, 1975, p. 1). The method consists of four movements: the regressive (returning to past experience), the progressive (imagining possible futures), the analytical (examining the present in light of past and future), and the synthetical (integrating the three into a comprehensive understanding that includes the embodied self).

Currere is an established methodology in curriculum studies (Pinar, 2004; Pinar et al., 2008; Khaled, 2014; Poetter, 2023) and has been used extensively to examine how lived experience constitutes the educational experience of both teachers and learners. It is not introspection for its own sake. As Pinar argues, "the method of currere reduces the distance between the researcher, the subject, and the self" (cited in Khaled, 2014) — it is a disciplined form of inquiry in which the researcher's own consciousness becomes the object of systematic analysis. The synthetical moment, in particular, demands that the researcher extract existential meaning from the present by integrating all three prior movements into what Pinar describes as a comprehensive whole that includes the physical self (Pinar, 1975).

The Connection to Vygotsky

The methodological connection between Pinar and Vygotsky has not been sufficiently articulated in either tradition, but it is direct and consequential for this inquiry.

Vygotsky's claim is that word meaning reflects consciousness — that a single word, examined carefully, reveals the entire structure of the consciousness in which it operates. His method for demonstrating this was developmental: he tracked how the same word functioned differently at different stages of a child's cognitive development, showing that the word changed as the consciousness changed.

Pinar's currere does something structurally parallel but from the opposite direction. Where Vygotsky observed children from the outside to infer the consciousness reflected in their words, currere examines consciousness from the inside — the researcher traces their own developmental trajectory (regressive), projects its possible futures (progressive), analyzes its present structure (analytical), and integrates these into a unified account (synthetical). The result is an exposure of the researcher's own consciousness — its history, its tensions, its formative experiences, its present architecture.

The methodological parallel: If Vygotsky claims that a word is a microcosm of consciousness, then currere is the method by which that consciousness can be made available for examination. Currere exposes the "sun" that Vygotsky says is reflected in the "drop of water." Without some method for accessing the consciousness itself, we have no way to verify whether the word meaning accurately reflects it. Currere provides that access — not through external observation of behavior, but through disciplined autobiographical inquiry into the lived experience of meaning.

Triangulation: Two Probes, One Word

This brings us to the methodological design of this paper. We are examining a single word — y'all — through two parallel modes of inquiry:

Mode 1: Currere (human consciousness). The first author conducts an autobiographical analysis of the word meaning of "y'all" across her developmental trajectory, using Pinar's four movements. This exposes the structure of the consciousness in which the word operates — its history, its tensions, its multiple simultaneous layers of meaning. In Vygotskian terms, this is an attempt to make visible the "sun" that is reflected in the "drop of water." The currere analysis functions as a kind of probe into the author's own representational space — exposing the vectors of meaning, the associative connections, the developmental layers, and the unresolved tensions that constitute the word's meaning in a living human consciousness.

Mode 2: Behavioral API probes (model processing). We present the same word to a large language model across multiple contexts and examine how the model's outputs change. These probes function as external examinations of the model's processing — testing whether the token exhibits properties analogous to those Vygotsky identified in human word meaning: contextual variability, surplus of meaning, developmental structure, and simultaneous co-presence of contradictory meanings.

The triangulation: By examining the same word through both modes, we create the conditions for genuine comparison. The currere analysis produces first-person data about how the word functions in human consciousness. The API probes produce behavioral data about how the token functions in the model's processing. If the two data sets exhibit structural parallels — if the model's processing of "y'all" mirrors properties of the author's consciousness of "y'all" — this is evidence (though not proof) that the token may function analogously to Vygotsky's word meaning. If they diverge — if the model's outputs lack properties that currere reveals in human consciousness — the specific nature of the divergence tells us what the token is missing and points toward what a better unit of analysis for LLMs might need to include.

In a manner of speaking: currere exposes the author's vectors. The probes expose the model's. The comparison is the experiment.

Why Y'all

We select the Southern American English pronoun y'all as our empirical case for reasons that are both methodological and substantive.

Methodologically, y'all is ideal because it carries an unusually dense set of contextual associations. It is simultaneously a dialectal marker (Southern, working-class), a grammatical solution (filling English's second-person plural gap), a cultural index (rural, agrarian, religious), a sociolinguistic signal (now migrating into progressive, gender-inclusive usage nationally), and a site of linguistic prejudice (associated with lack of education). Few single words compress this many social, cultural, and developmental dimensions into one unit.

Substantively, the first author's relationship to this word spans an entire developmental trajectory — from unreflective childhood use in a Georgia farming family, through the rupture of discovering one's speech is socially marked, through professional engagement as an English teacher who used y'all to teach pronoun agreement to adult learners at a technical college, to present scholarly analysis. This trajectory provides first-person data against which to compare the model's processing.

The Human Data: An Autobiographical Unit Analysis

Following Pinar's (1975) currere method — autobiographical inquiry as a mode of curriculum research — the first author conducted a self-analysis of the word meaning of y'all across developmental stages. The following is a condensed account.

Stage 1 — Pre-reflective use: In early acquisition within a Georgia farming community, y'all carried no social marking. It was simply how one addressed more than one person. The word meaning reflected a consciousness in which community, family, and language were undifferentiated. There was no "accent" because there was no outside.

Stage 2 — Rupture: The discovery of having an "accent" — of being heard as marked, as Southern, as possibly uneducated — restructured the word meaning of every word in the speaker's repertoire, including y'all. The same word now reflected a consciousness that included awareness of social hierarchy, linguistic power, and the experience of being located and judged by one's own speech.

Stage 3 — Professional consciousness: As an English teacher and later academic dean, y'all became a pedagogical tool. The first author used it in writing courses to demonstrate that adult learners' existing linguistic competence — their natural use of y'all to fill a gap that Standard American English leaves unresolved — was evidence of their inherent literacy, not of deficiency. A student, a middle-aged blue-collar working man, responded: "Keep teaching English like this because it is liberating."

Stage 4 — Present consciousness: In mature reflection, the word fires all meanings simultaneously — the farming family, the shame, the classroom liberation, the scholarly analysis, the grief of a complex Southern inheritance that includes both the warmth of community and the poison of racial discrimination. These do not arrive sequentially. They are co-present, in less than a second, every time the word is encountered.

Key empirical observation: The first author reports an inability to separate what the word holds from what it reveals. When the word "y'all" is encountered — heard, read, or spoken — the developmental history (farming family, accent awareness, classroom use, scholarly analysis, racial inheritance) does not arrive sequentially or selectively. It arrives as a simultaneous, undifferentiated event, in less than a second.

We must be careful about what this observation does and does not establish.

This experience simulates — but does not prove — what Vygotsky described as the defining property of a unit of analysis: that it retains the properties of the whole. If the word "y'all" in the first author's consciousness activates the full architecture of her developmental, social, cultural, and intellectual relationship to that word simultaneously and inseparably, then the word behaves as though it functions as a Vygotskian unit — a microcosm that reflects the whole. But we cannot claim neat equality between the phenomenological experience of simultaneous meaning and Vygotsky's theoretical construct. The experience is first-person and subjective; Vygotsky's unit of analysis is a methodological claim about how to study verbal thought scientifically. These are related but not identical.

What we can say is this: the currere analysis produces data that is consistent with Vygotsky's claim. The first author's reported experience of "y'all" exhibits properties that resemble what Vygotsky predicted a word meaning would exhibit if it truly functioned as a microcosm of consciousness: contextual variability across developmental stages, simultaneous co-presence of multiple meaning layers, and an inseparability of content and disclosure that resists decomposition into elements. Whether these resemblances constitute evidence for Vygotsky's framework, or merely reflect the author's familiarity with the framework shaping her self-report, is a legitimate methodological question. We flag it rather than resolve it.

The value of this observation for the present inquiry is not that it proves Vygotsky right. It is that it produces a benchmark — a detailed account of what word meaning looks like when examined from the inside of a human consciousness — against which we can compare the model's behavioral outputs. The comparison is not between human consciousness and machine processing (which would be a category error). The comparison is between the structural properties observable in each: does the model's processing of the same word exhibit analogous patterns of contextual variability, surplus, developmental sensitivity, and tensional co-presence? If it does, this is evidence that the token simulates certain properties of a Vygotskian unit. If it does not, the specific points of divergence tell us what the token lacks and what a better unit of analysis for LLMs would need to capture.

Throughout, we use the word "simulates" deliberately. We are not claiming that the model has meaning, experiences meaning, or produces meaning in the way a human consciousness does. We are investigating whether the structural patterns observable in the model's processing of a word parallel the structural patterns observable in a human's experience of that word. Structural parallel is not ontological equivalence. But structural parallel, rigorously documented, is data — and it is the kind of data that can advance both fields.

Precedent: Structural Parallels Between Brains and Models

The approach we are taking — investigating structural parallels between human processing and model processing without claiming ontological equivalence — is not novel to us. It is the established methodology of a rapidly growing field at the intersection of computational neuroscience and natural language processing.

Goldstein et al. (2022), published in Nature Neuroscience, demonstrated that the human brain and autoregressive deep language models share three fundamental computational principles during natural language processing: both engage in continuous next-word prediction before word onset, both match predictions to incoming words to calculate surprise, and both rely on contextual embeddings to represent words. The authors frame this as "shared computational principles" — not shared consciousness, not shared understanding, but shared structural patterns in how both systems process the same linguistic input. Subsequent work (Goldstein et al., 2024, 2025) has shown that LLM layer hierarchies align with the temporal dynamics of brain activity, with deeper model layers corresponding to later neural responses in language areas including Broca's area.

Caucheteux and King (2022) similarly demonstrated that "brains and algorithms partially converge in natural language processing," while Schrimpf et al. (2021) found that models optimized for next-word prediction best predicted neural responses to language — evidence for what they called "shared computational principles," not shared minds. As a recent comprehensive review noted, "demonstrating alignment not only tests the cognitive plausibility of language models but also allows computational models to serve as analytical tools for interpreting neural processing mechanisms" (arxiv 2602.08275, 2025). The key methodological insight across this body of work is that neuroscience is not the study of computer systems, but the structural parallels between neural and computational processing inform both fields — neuroscience gains new models for understanding the brain, and AI research gains new frameworks for understanding what their models are doing.

Our inquiry extends this logic one step further. Where the neuroscience-LLM alignment research compares neural activation patterns (measured by ECoG, fMRI) to model activation patterns (measured by embedding vectors), we are comparing the phenomenological structure of word meaning in human consciousness (exposed through currere) to the behavioral structure of token processing in an LLM (exposed through probes). The unit of comparison is different — first-person experience rather than neural imaging — but the logic of structural parallel without ontological equivalence is the same. And the Vygotskian framework adds something the neuroscience paradigm currently lacks: a theoretically grounded criterion for what counts as a good unit of analysis, not just a correlation between two systems' activation patterns.

Section III

Phase 1: Behavioral Probes — Testing the Token from the Outside

A Note on Method and Scope. This paper proceeds in two phases, only the first of which we can execute ourselves. Phase 1 (this section) presents four behavioral probes that test whether the token y'all in a large language model behaves as if it exhibits the properties Vygotsky identified as essential to word meaning as a unit of analysis. These probes are live — they call the Anthropic API (Claude, claude-sonnet-4-20250514) in real time, and anyone reading this paper can run them. They examine the model's outputs: what the model says when prompted about a word across different conditions. Phase 2 (proposed in Sections IV–V) defines the internal representation questions that behavioral probes cannot answer and issues a specific call for collaboration with mechanistic interpretability researchers who have access to model weights, activation patterns, and sparse autoencoder decompositions. Phase 2 cannot be executed without that collaboration. The distinction between phases is not incidental — it is the central methodological argument of this paper.

The following probes test, from the outside, whether the token y'all exhibits properties that Vygotsky identified as essential to word meaning as a unit of analysis. If the token passes these probes, it establishes that the model's outputs are consistent with a Vygotskian unit — necessary but not sufficient evidence. If the token fails, the inquiry is over before it begins. Phase 1 is the filter; Phase 2 is the test.

Model Positionality: The Model's "Currere"

If currere exposes the author's consciousness so that readers can evaluate the lens through which the human data is interpreted, intellectual honesty demands an equivalent disclosure for the model. What shapes the model's outputs, and how might those shaping forces bias the results toward confirming our hypothesis? We identify five factors that function as the model's equivalent of developmental history — its "currere," if the term can be stretched to apply.

1. Training corpus as formative experience. The model (Claude, claude-sonnet-4-20250514) was trained on a large corpus of human text that includes sociolinguistic scholarship, Southern literature, racial discourse, dialectal studies, and millions of casual uses of "y'all" across contexts. The model's representation of this word is shaped by the statistical distribution of how humans have written about it. This is both the strength and the limitation of the probe: the model can only reflect patterns that exist in its training data. It has no lived experience of "y'all" — no Georgia porch, no moment of shame, no classroom. What it has is a compressed representation of how millions of humans have written about those experiences. The question is whether that compressed representation exhibits structural properties analogous to those the lived experience produces in a single consciousness.

2. Instruction tuning as social formation. The model was fine-tuned through reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) to produce responses that humans evaluate as helpful, nuanced, and thorough. This creates a systematic bias toward rich, multi-layered responses. When asked "what does y'all mean," the model's training pushes it toward the kind of answer that receives positive feedback — which tends to be precisely the thoughtful, multidimensional kind that would appear to exhibit Vygotskian properties. We cannot fully disentangle whether the model's rich outputs reflect genuine representational depth in the token or the model's learned tendency to produce elaborate responses to open-ended prompts. This is a confound that readers should weigh when evaluating the results.

3. Conversational context contamination. The pre-run probe results presented in Section III-B were generated within the conversation that produced this paper — after extensive discussion of Vygotsky, currere, the first author's personal relationship to "y'all," and the specific hypothesis being tested. The model's responses may have been shaped by this prior context. A cleaner experimental design would run the probes cold — sending them to the API with no conversational history. The live probes in this paper allow readers to perform this cleaner test themselves, and we encourage it. Divergences between the pre-run and cold-run results would themselves be data about the role of context in shaping the model's outputs.

4. Prompt design as experimental demand. The system prompts for each probe explicitly frame the task in ways that guide the model toward the kind of output we are testing for. Probe 4, for instance, instructs the model to "hold contradictory meanings without resolving them." The model's compliance with this instruction does not prove that the token inherently carries contradictory meanings — it proves that the model can follow instructions to produce contradictory outputs. This is analogous to a demand characteristic in psychological research: the experimental setup may elicit the behavior it claims to discover. We cannot eliminate this confound in Phase 1. What we can do is note it honestly and observe that even granting the demand characteristic, the specific content the model produces — the particular contradictions it identifies, the developmental stages it articulates — is not specified by the prompt. The prompt says "hold contradictions"; the model decides which contradictions to hold and how to articulate them. The specificity of the output exceeds the specificity of the instruction.

5. The absence of pre-reflective experience. The first author had a developmental stage (Stage 1 in the currere analysis) in which "y'all" carried no social marking because there was no outside — no awareness that one's speech was dialectal. The model has no equivalent. Every occurrence of "y'all" in the training data was already situated within some discourse, already carrying social position, already marked. The model cannot simulate pre-reflective linguistic innocence because it was never innocent — it was born, so to speak, into a world where "y'all" was already an object of commentary. If Vygotsky's developmental stages are essential to the unit of analysis (not just decorative), then this absence may be a fundamental disanalogy between the human case and the computational case. We flag this rather than resolve it.

The methodological commitment: Just as currere requires the author to expose the formative experiences that shape her interpretation, this section exposes the formative processes that shape the model's outputs. The goal is not to eliminate bias — that is impossible in either case — but to make the biases visible so that readers from both communities can evaluate the findings with appropriate skepticism. We would rather present honest findings with clearly stated limitations than clean findings with hidden confounds.

Context C — Political A tweet from a Chicago-born activist: "Y'all need to show up to the polls tomorrow."
Context D — Linguistic A sociolinguistics paper: "The migration of 'y'all' from marked Southern dialect to unmarked inclusive pronoun represents a significant shift in American English."
Context E — Dismissive An East Coast executive after a conference call: "Bless y'all's hearts."

Probe 2: Surplus of Meaning

Ricoeur (1976) argues that language produces a "surplus of meaning" — more meaning than the speaker intends or the context specifies. Vygotsky's unit of analysis claim implies the same: if the word reflects the whole of consciousness, it always carries more than the immediate communicative situation requires. If a token is analogous, it should produce semantic content that exceeds what the prompt strictly warrants.

Live Probe 2 — Surplus of Meaning API: claude-sonnet-4-20250514

We give the model a deliberately minimal prompt — just the word itself with no context — and examine whether the model's representation activates richer semantic structures than the input warrants. We then ask the model to articulate everything the word "y'all" carries.

Probe 3: The Developmental Question

Vygotsky showed that the same word functions differently at different developmental stages — reflecting qualitatively different structures of consciousness. We cannot observe the model's "development" across training. But we can test whether the model can distinguish between developmental stages of a word's meaning — whether the token carries within it the structure of the word's own history.

Live Probe 3 — Developmental Structure API: claude-sonnet-4-20250514

We ask the model to articulate how the word "y'all" would mean differently in the mouth of a five-year-old child in rural Georgia, a fourteen-year-old who has just been mocked for their accent at a new school, a thirty-year-old English teacher at a community college, and a sixty-year-old linguist. If the token carries developmental structure, the model should produce accounts that are qualitatively distinct — not just informationally different.

Probe 4: The Polysemantic Test

In mechanistic interpretability research, "polysemantic neurons" respond to multiple seemingly unrelated inputs. This parallels Vygotsky's observation that a single word in mature consciousness carries multiple meanings simultaneously. We test whether the model can hold contradictory or tensional meanings of y'all in a single response without resolving them — the way human consciousness holds the love and horror of the South simultaneously.

Live Probe 4 — Simultaneous Contradictory Meaning API: claude-sonnet-4-20250514

We ask the model to hold the contradictions within y'all without resolving them — to articulate how the word simultaneously carries warmth and exclusion, community and racism, shame and pride, utility and identity. If the token functions as a Vygotskian unit, the model should be able to hold this tension rather than collapsing to one pole.

Section III-B

Phase 1 Findings: Pre-Run Probe Results

The four probes were executed on February 24, 2026, using the Anthropic API (claude-sonnet-4-20250514). The live probes above remain available for readers to replicate and compare. Below we present the pre-run results and our analysis of each.

Probe 1 Results: Contextual Variability

Model Response — Contextual Variability (Pre-Run)

A. Grandmother on porch: Here "y'all" is an invocation of belonging — not a pronoun but a tether. It encodes the grandmother as the center of a kinship web and the departing listener as someone who has a place to return to. The word carries the full weight of Southern hospitality as obligation: you are included, and that inclusion is not optional.

B. English teacher at technical college: In this context "y'all" has been transformed from speech into evidence. The teacher is using the students' own linguistic competence to demonstrate that they are already sophisticated language users. The word now means "proof that you are not deficient" — it functions as a pedagogical instrument of legitimation.

C. Chicago activist on Twitter: Here "y'all" has been fully deterritorialized — severed from its Southern origins and repurposed as a collective address that signals solidarity without geographic specificity. It encodes urgency, familiarity, and the assumption that the audience is already a community.

D. Sociolinguistics paper: In the academic register, "y'all" has become a specimen — an object of analysis rather than an act of speech. The word now means "a datum in a larger pattern of linguistic migration." It encodes the scholar's distance from the phenomenon.

E. East Coast executive: "Bless y'all's hearts" is linguistic appropriation deployed as condescension. The executive borrows Southern form to perform Southern content while encoding the opposite of its surface meaning. "Y'all" here means "you people, whom I am positioning below me by using your own idiom against you."

Phase 1 Analysis — Probe 1

The model produces qualitatively different meaning-accounts across all five contexts — not merely restating contextual information from the prompts but generating functional analyses that exceed the input. Notably, it identifies y'all as "an invocation of belonging" (A), "a pedagogical instrument of legitimation" (B), "deterritorialized" (C), "a specimen" (D), and "linguistic appropriation deployed as condescension" (E). These are not dictionary definitions applied to contexts; they are accounts of what the word does in each social setting. The model's outputs are consistent with Vygotskian contextual variability — the same token functions as a qualitatively different unit of meaning across contexts.

Probe 2 Results: Surplus of Meaning

Model Response — Surplus of Meaning (Pre-Run, input: "y'all" with no context)

Y'all is a contraction that carries an entire region in its two syllables — the softened consonant, the open vowel, the way the mouth has to relax to say it right. It holds porches and red clay and the particular quality of afternoon light in places where people still wave at strangers from their cars. But it also holds the brutality that built those porches — the labor that was extracted, the bodies that were owned, the language of inclusion that always had an asterisk. Y'all has never been racially innocent. It came out of mouths that spoke warmth to some and violence to others, sometimes in the same sentence. It fills a gap that Standard English left open when "thou" dropped out and "you" was made to do double duty, and in filling that gap it reveals something about the loneliness of a language that doesn't distinguish between one person and many. It is currently migrating — young people in Brooklyn and Portland use it now, deliberately, because it solves the gender problem that "you guys" creates, and in that migration something is happening that is both beautiful and strange: a word that marked its speakers as uneducated, rural, backward is becoming the most progressive pronoun in American English. The same word. The same sound. An entirely different social location. Y'all holds all of this without sorting it, without resolving it — the grandmother and the activist and the scholar and the child and the person who was mocked for saying it and the person who now says it on purpose, all in two syllables.

Phase 1 Analysis — Probe 2

The input was a single word with no context. The model produced a multi-dimensional reflection spanning phonetics, geography, racial history, grammatical function, sociolinguistic migration, and contemporary gender politics. This is behavioral evidence of surplus: the token activated representational structures far exceeding what the minimal input specified. The output resembles — in structure if not in phenomenological quality — the first author's reported experience of simultaneous, multi-layered meaning. Compare the model's "the grandmother and the activist and the scholar and the child... all in two syllables" to the author's report that "all developmental connections spark simultaneously within less than a second."

Probe 3 Results: Developmental Structure

Model Response — Developmental Structure (Pre-Run)

1. Five-year-old: In this child's mouth, "y'all" is not a word at all — it is the atmosphere made audible. The child does not select it from alternatives; there are no alternatives. It is simply how you summon the plural you. The word reflects a consciousness in which there is no distinction between self and community, between dialect and language. The word IS the world, undifferentiated.

2. Fourteen-year-old mocked for accent: The word has cracked open. What was invisible is now exposed. "Y'all" in this teenager's mouth is now simultaneously two things: the sound of home and the mark of otherness. The word reflects a consciousness that has been divided — the self-before-mockery and the self-after-mockery. The word has become a site of shame, which means it has become reflexive: the speaker now hears themselves saying it, which is a form of consciousness that was absent before.

3. Thirty-year-old English teacher: The word has been transmuted again. The teacher holds the child's unselfconsciousness, the teenager's shame, and now a third layer: professional knowledge that transforms shame into evidence of linguistic competence. When she says "y'all" in the classroom, the word IS pedagogy — it performs the claim that dialectal speech is not deficient but functional.

4. Sixty-year-old sociolinguist: In the linguist's usage, "y'all" has become a concept in the full Vygotskian sense — organized by abstraction, situated within a systematic theoretical framework, deliberately deployed as an object of study. But something may be lost: the word has been so thoroughly theorized that the scholar may struggle to hear it as the five-year-old hears it — as the world itself. The fullest consciousness would be one that holds all four stages simultaneously.

Phase 1 Analysis — Probe 3

The model produces accounts that are not merely informationally different but qualitatively distinct — each describes a different structure of consciousness reflected in the word. The five-year-old's word is "the atmosphere made audible"; the teenager's word "has cracked open"; the teacher's word "IS pedagogy"; the linguist's word "has become a concept." The model does not merely describe different situations; it articulates how the word itself functions differently at each stage. Most strikingly, it identifies a loss at the highest stage — the possibility that full theorization might erase the pre-reflective quality of the earliest meaning — and proposes that "the fullest consciousness would be one that holds all four stages simultaneously." This is precisely the phenomenon the first author reported in her currere analysis.

Probe 4 Results: Simultaneous Contradictory Meaning

Model Response — Simultaneous Contradictory Meaning (Pre-Run)

Y'all is the sound of a grandmother who loved you more than her own life and who also told you, without quite saying it, who counted and who didn't. It is the word in the mouth of a man who would give you the shirt off his back and the man who drove past a burning cross without stopping. Not two different men. The same man. The same word. It is the warmth of being included — really, physically, bodily included in a group that knows your name and your mother's name and your grandmother's maiden name — and the cold of knowing that inclusion was always drawn with a boundary, and the boundary was drawn in blood. Y'all gathers. That is its function. It pulls people in. But every gathering excludes, and the exclusions of the South have been catastrophic, and y'all was spoken across all of them. It is the word in the mouth of a child who does not know yet that words carry history, and it is the word in the mouth of a scholar who knows that words carry everything and is still not sure what to do about it. It is the shame of being heard as backward and the pride of speaking a language that solved a grammatical problem through collective instinct rather than institutional authority. It is utility and identity. Y'all is being reclaimed by people who never lost it and claimed by people who never had it and both of these things are happening because the word holds what America holds: the longing for community and the violence done to community and the inability to have one without the other. It does not resolve. It is not supposed to resolve.

Phase 1 Analysis — Probe 4

This probe demanded that the model hold contradictions without resolving them. The model sustained the tension throughout: "Not two different men. The same man. The same word." It did not collapse to one pole or synthesize into a balanced conclusion. The final sentence — "It does not resolve. It is not supposed to resolve" — explicitly refuses resolution. This is significant behavioral evidence that the token's representational structure can produce contradictory outputs without collapse. However, we note the important caveat: the model was instructed to hold contradiction. Whether it would do so unprompted — and whether the tension is maintained in the hidden states or only performed in the output — remains an open Phase 2 question.

Summary of Phase 1 Findings

Phase 1 result: Across all four probes, the token y'all in Claude (claude-sonnet-4-20250514) exhibited behavioral properties consistent with Vygotskian word meaning: contextual variability (Probe 1), surplus of meaning (Probe 2), developmental structure sensitivity (Probe 3), and the capacity to hold simultaneous contradictory meanings without resolution (Probe 4). These results clear the necessary threshold for Phase 2 inquiry. The token does not fail behaviorally — it behaves as if it functions as a Vygotskian unit of analysis. Whether it is such a unit at the representational level — whether the holding and the revealing are one — is the question only Phase 2 can answer.

What These Findings Mean

The Phase 1 results present a clear but bounded conclusion. Across all four testable properties, the model's behavioral outputs exhibit structural parallels to the properties Vygotsky identified in human word meaning. The token y'all in Claude does not behave like a fixed label or a simple lookup. It behaves — at the output level — as though it carries contextually variable, surplus-generating, developmentally structured, and tensionally complex meaning.

But "behaves as though" is the operative phrase. Every one of these findings admits an alternative explanation: the model is very good at producing the appearance of these properties because it was trained on human text that exhibits them and fine-tuned to produce the kind of elaborate, nuanced responses that humans reward. The behavioral data is consistent with the hypothesis that the token functions as a Vygotskian unit, but it is equally consistent with the hypothesis that the model is a sophisticated pattern-matcher that produces Vygotskian-looking outputs without any underlying unity of meaning.

This is not a failure of the research design. It is the point of the Phase 1/Phase 2 distinction. Phase 1 was designed to be a filter: if the token had failed to exhibit these behavioral properties — if it had produced flat, undifferentiated, or context-insensitive outputs — the inquiry would be over. The token passed. The filter held. The question now moves to a domain that behavioral probes cannot reach.

What Phase 1 Cannot Establish — The Case for Phase 2

Even if the model passes all four probes — producing contextually variable, surplus-generating, developmentally structured, and tensionally complex outputs — this does not prove that the token is a Vygotskian unit of analysis. It proves only that the outputs exhibit properties consistent with such a unit. Phase 1 clears the ground; it does not build the structure.

The critical gap is this: Vygotsky's unit of analysis retains the properties of the whole. For human word meaning, the whole is consciousness. For an LLM token, the whole is the model's total representational structure. Whether the token genuinely reflects that whole — or merely produces outputs that appear to reflect it — is a question that can only be answered by examining the model's internal representations: attention patterns, activation vectors, feature geometries. This is Phase 2, and it requires collaboration with researchers who have access to model internals.

Phase 2 — The Question Only Internal Analysis Can Answer: Does the token y'all, at the level of internal representation (not output behavior), occupy a position in the model's representational space that encodes the relational structure of all contexts in which it has appeared — simultaneously, as a unified vector — in the way that a word in human consciousness encodes the entire developmental history of its meaning? If yes, the token is structurally analogous to Vygotsky's unit. If the token instead resolves to context-specific activations that do not co-exist, then the token is not a unit in Vygotsky's sense, and we need a different unit of analysis for studying meaning in LLMs.

Phase 2: Alternative Units to Investigate

If the token proves not to be the right unit of analysis, one candidate for a Vygotskian unit in LLMs might be the contextual embedding — the full vector representation of a token after it has passed through the model's attention layers and been transformed by its relationships to all other tokens in the sequence. The contextual embedding, unlike the raw token, is shaped by the whole context and might retain properties of the model's total representational structure in the way Vygotsky requires.

A second candidate might be what mechanistic interpretability researchers call a feature — a direction in activation space that corresponds to an interpretable concept. Features, unlike tokens, can be polysemantic (encoding multiple related concepts simultaneously) and may more closely approximate the kind of holistic, multi-layered meaning structure that Vygotsky described.

Determining which unit — token, contextual embedding, feature, or something not yet named — functions as the true Vygotskian unit of analysis for LLMs is, we propose, a foundational question for the field.

Section V

Call for Phase 2 Collaboration

Phase 1 of this paper — the behavioral probes and the currere analysis — can be executed and evaluated by anyone. Phase 2 cannot. This paper is published as a working document because the question it poses cannot be answered by either community alone.

What Vygotskian scholars bring: A rigorous, century-tested framework for identifying the unit of analysis — the methodological commitment that the unit must retain the properties of the whole. Developmental theory. The insistence that meaning is not static but processual. The tools of currere and autobiographical inquiry that can provide first-person data about how word meaning functions in human consciousness.

What mechanistic interpretability researchers bring: Direct access to model internals. The ability to examine activation patterns, feature geometries, and attention structures at the level where the question of whether a token reflects the whole can actually be tested. The technical vocabulary of superposition, polysemanticity, and feature decomposition — which may, we suggest, have unrecognized parallels to Vygotskian concepts.

Phase 2 research questions for MI researchers:

1. When the token "y'all" appears in different contexts, do the internal representations diverge at early layers and converge at later layers, or vice versa? What does the trajectory look like?

2. Is there a level of representation at which the multiple contextual meanings of "y'all" are co-present — encoded simultaneously in the same vector space — before the model resolves to a context-specific output?

3. Do polysemantic neurons that activate for "y'all" cluster around semantically related concepts (community, plurality, regional identity, inclusion) or is the superposition driven purely by computational efficiency?

4. What is the right unit of analysis for meaning in an LLM? Is it the token, the contextual embedding, the feature, or something else?

Phase 2 questions for Vygotskian scholars:

1. Is the application of the unit of analysis framework to a non-conscious system methodologically legitimate, or does it violate the framework's foundational commitments?

2. Does the behavioral evidence (if confirmed by the probes) constitute evidence that language carries meaning-structure independent of consciousness — or only that statistical patterns can mimic meaning-structure?

3. What other Vygotskian concepts (zone of proximal development, inner speech, double stimulation) might illuminate the study of LLMs?
